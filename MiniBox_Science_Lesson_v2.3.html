The problem

Your lungs never fully empty.

Even after you blow out as hard as you can, a large pocket of air stays trapped inside. That leftover air is where the answers hide: air trapping, hyperinflation, stiff restricted lungs. You just can't get at it.

This hidden volume is exactly what a full lung test needs to measure.
Why it's hard

A spirometer can't see it.

Spirometry only measures air that moves in and out of the mouth. The trapped air never moves, so to a spirometer it may as well not exist. That's the gap the MiniBox fills.

So how do you measure air that refuses to move?
The idea

Connect it to a box you've already measured.

Here's the move. Hook the lungs up to a rigid container whose size you know down to the milliliter: 16.3 liters. Now the mystery air and a known amount of air share one sealed space.

A patient just breathes into it. No booth. No gas.
The trick

Snap a valve shut, for an instant.

A valve closes for a few thousandths of a second. In that frozen moment the lung and the box are one sealed pocket of air, and the patient's breath gives it the tiniest squeeze.

Now the trapped air has to push back. And that is what we read.
The whole secret

Squeeze it. More air gives more.

You have felt this with a sealed syringe. One packed with air gives a lot when you press it. One with barely any air barely moves. Same push, different give. Tap to press both.

The syringe with more air gives further. That "give" is the fingerprint of the hidden volume, and it is the whole measurement.
Solve it

You know the box. You felt the pushback. Solve for the lung.

A 200-year-old gas law ties pressure and volume together. The box's share is known, the pushback is measured, so the only unknown left is the trapped lung volume. The machine does the algebra in a blink.

That flow change, F₀ to Fᶜ, is exactly the pushback on the classic MiniBox diagram.
The positioning

This is still plethysmography. Just un-caged.

Plethysmography means measuring hidden volume against a known box. The old body box seals the whole patient inside the known box. The MiniBox keeps the box on the desk and runs only the breath into it. Same physics. Tap to compare.

Reads the same as the body box in head-to-head studies. No cabin, no gas, nobody sealed in.
See it work

Now run the LVM yourself.

The measurement is the LVM, the lung volume measurement, and it needs six clean breaths. Every breath is captured through a valve that closes for a few milliseconds, so brief the patient never feels it. Once six good breaths are in, the algorithm locks the number. Pick a patient and press run.
