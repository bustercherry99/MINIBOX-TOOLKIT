What It Measures
Complete set of 30+ parameters, fully ATS/ERS compliant
🤏 Spirometry (FVC)
FEV1, FVC, FEV1/FVC, FEF 25-75, PEF, FEV6, MVV, SVC, and more. Pre and post bronchodilator. Methacholine challenge. Provocation testing. 6-Minute Walk Test.
🍧 Lung Volumes (LVM)
TLC, FRC, RV, VC, IC, ERV — same volumes as body plethysmography. Measures trapped air that gas dilution methods miss in obstructive disease. GLI reference values standard.

DLCO, VA, DLCO/VA (KCO). Single-breath method. Fully automatic. Differentiates damage from COVID vs. COPD vs. asthma. Identifies ILD, emphysema, pulmonary vascular disease.

Patient Coaching Quick Reference
Setup, technique, and what to watch for during each test
ⓘ
Universal setup for LVM and DLCO: HHU attached to MiniBox+. Adjust device height for patient. Lips sealed tightly over mouthpiece, teeth over mouthpiece, nose clip applied.
FVC
Forced Vital Capacity
HHU detached from MiniBox+ for zeroing — then handed to patient. ATS requires 3 to 8 efforts.
Setup
  • 1
    Detach HHU from MiniBox+ and allow to calibrate (zeroing).
  • 2
    Hand HHU to patient. Seal lips around mouthpiece, teeth over it, apply nose clip.
Coaching the Patient
  • •
    Start with 2 to 3 normal relaxed breaths.
  • •
    Deep inhale, then immediate forced fast exhale.
  • •
    Squeeze ALL air out until the wheel turns GREEN.
  • •
    Deep breath back in to close the flow volume loop.
  • •
    Coach them to "BLAST" not just "blow." Prompt the sharp start — no hesitation at top of inhale.
LVM
Lung Volume Measurement
HHU attached to MiniBox+. Must be performed after FVC. Counter must reach SIX successful events.
Setup
  • 1
    Attach HHU to MiniBox+. Adjust device height for patient.
  • 2
    Patient seals lips and applies nose clip.
Coaching the Patient
  • •
    Relaxed normal breathing for about one minute. Keep breaths below 1 Liter.
  • •
    Once counter turns GREEN and reads "6," on the NEXT breath take a slow deep inhale to full capacity.
  • •
    Breathe out slow, squeeze all air out until the line hits plateau (empty).
  • •
    Take a breath in and come off mouthpiece.
  • •
    Watch for downward or upward sloping graph — indicates air leak.
DLCO
Diffusing Capacity
HHU attached. Minimum 2, maximum 5 tests. Allow 4 minutes between tests for gas clearance.
Setup
  • 1
    Attach HHU to MiniBox+. Adjust height. Patient seals lips, applies nose clip.
  • 2
    Start on an inhale and complete FOUR normal tidal breaths.
Coaching the Patient
  • •
    Patient exhales fully on the 4th breath.
  • •
    Wait for device to click (valve closes), then take a fast deep breath in to TLC.
  • •
    Inhalation must reach above the GREEN dotted line. Patient should inhale 85% of VC within 4 seconds.
  • •
    Hold breath for 8 to 10 seconds.
  • •
    Once wheel turns GREEN, exhale completely to empty. Then inhale and come off mouthpiece.

Common Failure Reasons & Fixes
FVC — "Don't hesitate, exhale faster"
Patient paused at the top of the inhalation (Evol exceeded 5% of FVC). Fix: coach them to immediately blast the air out the moment they reach peak inhalation — zero hesitation.
FVC — End-of-test criteria not found
Patient stopped exhaling before the plateau was reached. Fix: "Keep going, blow out every last bit of air." Common in COPD patients who have prolonged expiratory time.
LVM — TGV line not established
Tidal breathing was irregular before the maneuver. Fix: delete this test result (manual specifically recommends it). Allow patient to settle and breathe normally before restarting.
DLCO — Couldn't find stable parameters
The 4 required tidal breaths were skipped or unsteady. Fix: reinforce the sequence. Do NOT rush the patient into the maneuver. Wait the full 4 minutes before repeating.

Interpreting PFT Results
A stepwise clinical approach authored by Jeffrey Weiss, MD — Weiss Medical Allergy, Asthma & Immunology
ⓘ
The MiniBox+ automatically generates an ATS/ERS-based interpretation and decision tree with every report. The following is the clinical logic behind it. Physician review is always required.
1
Look at spirometry first. Normal or abnormal FEV1. If abnormal, look for other signs of an obstructive ventilatory defect: low FEF 25-75 and low FEV1/FVC.
2
If FVC is low, this could be a sign of a restrictive ventilatory defect. Check PEF to evaluate the effort of the maneuver.
3
If clinical history suggests asthma, administer a bronchodilator and check for a bronchodilator response (12% improvement in FEV1 or 20% improvement in FEF25-75). You can also perform methacholine challenge — a 15 to 20% decrease in FEV1 from the provocative dose is significant.
4
If FEV1 is normal but you're still concerned, check Lung Volumes next. If RV is elevated, this suggests air trapping — which could indicate an obstructive ventilatory defect (e.g. asthma).
5
If lung volumes show a decrease in TLC, this is indicative of a restrictive ventilatory defect.
6
If restriction is confirmed, check DLCO to determine the cause. Normal DLCO suggests extra-parenchymal processes (outside the lungs) such as a neuromuscular disorder. Low DLCO suggests an intra-parenchymal process (within the lungs) such as interstitial lung disease, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, sarcoidosis, or emphysema.
🌟
DLCO Clinical Pearls
Low DLCO — Think
Emphysema, Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD), Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis, Sarcoidosis, Pulmonary Vascular disease, Anemia, Early emphysema, Early ILD. Also present in COVID-related lung damage and COPD.
High DLCO — Think
Asthma, Obesity, Polycythemia, Intrapulmonary Hemorrhage. A high DLCO in context of obstruction often points toward asthma rather than emphysema.
Normal Spirometry + Low DLCO
May suggest Anemia, Pulmonary Vascular Disorders, Early ILD, or Early Emphysema. Lung volumes are normal but gas transfer is impaired — catch this early before spirometry changes.
Emphysema Pattern
Can present with airflow obstruction on PFTs AND low DLCO simultaneously. This combination is a key differentiator from asthma, where DLCO is typically normal or elevated.
💡
Why LVM matters for allergists and pulmonologists: Spirometry can appear normal even when air trapping is occurring. An elevated RV (residual volume) on the LVM test is the earliest indicator of obstructive disease — especially in asthma where the FEV1 may still be within normal range. This is a compelling reason to add LVM to every spirometry encounter.

Connection Type

Data Flow: What Goes In and What Comes Out
→ EMR to MiniBox (ORMO01 Order Message)
The EMR sends patient demographic and order information to the MiniBox before the test begins. This pre-populates the patient record automatically.
Required fields: Patient ID, Patient Name, Date of Birth, Sex, Message Type (ORM^O01), Universal Service ID (e.g., PFT13^COMPLETE PULMONARY FUNCTION TEST).
Valid test order codes:
PFT13^COMPLETE PULMONARY FUNCTION TEST → FVC + LVM + DLCO
PulmOneSPR^Pulm One Spirometry → FVC only
PulmOneSVC^Pulm One Slow Vital → SVC only
PulmOneMVV^Pulm One Voluntary → MVV only
PFT Full → FVC + LVM + DLCO
← MiniBox to EMR (ORUR01 Results Message)
After testing, the MiniBox automatically sends results back to the EMR. No manual data entry required.
What is transmitted per parameter: Best measured value, Predicted value, % Predicted, Lower Limit of Normal (LLN), Upper Limit of Normal (ULN), and Z-Score.
Also included: Full visit summary PDF report encoded in Base64 within the ORUR01 message — OR stored on a network drive with the filename embedded in the message. Pre and Post bronchodilator values sent as separate OBX segments.

Security Architecture
MDS2 Disclosure — SW Versions 3.2.0.0 to 3.3.0.0 — January 30, 2025
Data Encryption
Patient data stored in an encrypted SQLite database on the tablet's internal drive. Local encryption via Windows BitLocker. Data partitioned separately from the operating system.
User Authentication
Role-based access control (Admin and General roles). Password minimum 8 characters with uppercase, lowercase, digit, and special character required. Account locked after 5 failed attempts. Passwords expire after 90 days.
Auto Logoff
Automatic logoff after 15 minutes of inactivity (configurable). System returns to home screen. Configurable with assistance from PulmOne Customer Support.
Audit Logging
Audit log records: successful and unsuccessful logins, user creation/modification/deletion, data import/export via USB, HL7 data transmission, and software updates. Log exportable via USB. Requires admin privileges to access.
Kiosk Mode
Device operates in Windows Kiosk Mode by default. Operators can only access the MiniBox application. Windows admin password required to exit Kiosk mode and install any third-party software.
Network Security
Windows Firewall enabled. Wi-Fi with WPA/WPA2 authentication. No internet connection required for PFT testing. Device does not communicate with external networks outside of HL7 to the local EMR server.
⚠
HL7 data in transit is not encrypted. PulmOne recommends HL7 communication occur within the practice's internal network. The device does not transmit ePHI over the internet. Full MDS2 disclosure document available on request from PulmOne (PUO-DOCXXXXX, Jan 30, 2025).

CO and CH₄ Concentration Low
Work through each step in order before escalating to a repair
Step 1 — Verify Gas Mixture
Confirm the tank contains the correct mixture:

• 0.3% Methane (CH₄)
• 0.3% Carbon Monoxide (CO)
• 21% Oxygen (O₂)
• Balanced Nitrogen (N₂)
• 2% Accuracy Medical Grade
Step 2 — Check Tank Pressure & Volume
Verify both gauges on the regulator:

• Left gauge (Pressure): 70–75 PSI
• Right gauge (Volume): Above 500 PSI

If either reading is out of range, replace or refill the tank before proceeding.
Step 3 — Purge / Bleed the Gas Line
1. Turn off the gas tank completely.
2. Use the white knob to decrease pressure as far as it will go.
3. Disconnect the gas tubing from the regulator: press up the tiny black O-ring, then pull the tubing downward.
Step 4 — Cubic App Recalibration (Pre-2023 Devices Only)
If steps 1–3 don't resolve and device is older than 2023, use Cubic's app to recalibrate the gas analyzer. Watch the Cubic app training video first.

If the app still shows low CO and CH₄: device must come in for repair.

Common Test Failure Reasons & Fixes
Patient coaching corrections for FVC, LVM, and DLCO failures
FVC — Don't hesitate, exhale faster
Patient paused at the top of inhalation (Evol exceeded 5% of FVC). Fix: coach them to immediately blast air out the moment they reach peak inhalation — zero hesitation.
FVC — End-of-test criteria not found
Patient stopped exhaling before plateau was reached. Fix: "Keep going, blow out every last bit of air." Common in COPD patients with prolonged expiratory time.
LVM — TGV line not established
Tidal breathing was irregular before the maneuver. Fix: delete this test result (manual specifically recommends it). Let patient settle, then restart.
DLCO — Couldn't find stable parameters
The 4 required tidal breaths were skipped or unsteady. Fix: reinforce the full sequence. Do NOT rush the patient. Wait the full 4 minutes before repeating.

    Aging Device Assessment
    Field triage guide for devices 5+ years old — know what to look for before the device goes down
    🕒 Device Expected Lifetime: 10 Years (per manufacturer)
    Devices approaching or past this threshold should be proactively reviewed. Most component failures do not give advance warning. The goal is to get ahead of downtime before it affects patient care or billing.
    HIGH RISK DLCO Gas System
    • Gas Analyzer error / DLCO unavailable

      CO or CH4 analyzer failure. Device hangs on "Preparing for DLCO measurement." Requires PulmOne service or device replacement.

    • Rapid DLCO tank depletion

      E-tank empties in days. Common after 7+ years. Valve replacement does not resolve it — suspect internal manifold leak. Close tank valve at end of day as interim measure.

    • Recurring DLCO calibration failure

      Weekly 3L syringe check failing to return 0 ± 1 mL/min/mmHg despite correct technique. Indicates gas analyzer drift or partial valve degradation.

    • Low CO / CH4 concentration readings

      Abnormally low values with confirmed gas supply at 4 to 5 bar secondary pressure. Points to gas analyzer degradation rather than supply issue.

    HIGH RISK Occluder Valve / Interrupter
    • Valve stuck open or closed

      No test results displayed at all. Listed in the user manual as a confirmed failure mode requiring device replacement. The fast interrupter valve is the core mechanical component for LVM and LVC.

    • Valve not actuating fast enough

      LVM tests return repeated alerts: "not enough qualified valve closure events." Patients feel resistance or a delay at breath interruption. Valve actuator wearing out.

    • LVM tests consistently failing or inconsistent

      Can't obtain acceptable maneuvers across multiple patients. Often precedes full valve failure. Staff notes "it never used to do this."

    MONITOR Tablet / Control Unit
    • Battery swelling / case damage

      Battery degradation causes gas expansion inside the battery, physically pushing the tablet case cover off. Immediate safety issue. Replace tablet immediately. Common in units 5+ years under heavy daily use.

    • Tablet losing charge rapidly

      Requires constant wall connection. Battery cycle life exhausted. Replacement tablets available from PulmOne — lead time approximately one month, plan accordingly.

    • Software updates no longer supported

      Older Dell tablets may be locked out of Windows updates required for current MiniBox software versions. Creates compliance and performance gaps over time.

    • Touchscreen sluggish or unresponsive

      Staff defaults to keyboard or mouse. Normal wear on aging units. Impacts testing workflow but does not affect result accuracy.

    MONITOR CPU Cable / Connectivity
    • CPU cable triggering DLCO / comm errors

      "Communication with device has been lost" triggered by bending or flexing the main USB CPU cable. Outer coating cracking or brittle. Full DLCO failure results from cable flex alone — not a gas analyzer problem.

    • HHU disconnects during testing

      Handheld unit loses connection mid-session. Cable from HHU to main unit shows cracking at connectors or poor seating at the back panel.

    • USB ports not recognizing peripherals

      Flash drives, printers, or keyboards not consistently recognized. Internal USB port wear over years of daily use.

    HIGH RISK Flow Sensor / Calibration
    • Daily calibration failing repeatedly

      3L syringe calibration check fails consistently despite correct technique. Manual states: contact your PulmOne representative. Results may already be clinically inaccurate before outright failure occurs.

    • FVC / FEV1 values drifting unexpectedly

      Returning patients showing drops not explained clinically. Sensor drift in aging bidirectional flow sensors can produce misleading results before the device shows any obvious error.

    • Extended warm-up time

      Device taking well beyond 10 minutes to reach thermal stability. Temperature sensor degradation. Functional but a leading indicator of broader system wear.

    MONITOR Handheld Unit / Main Body
    • HHU not seating or locking

      Locking pin or groove worn down. Unit wobbles or disengages during docked DLCO testing. Creates both test quality and patient safety concerns.

    • Blue power light flickering or absent

      Indicator light around HHU connection unreliable despite confirmed power. Manual lists fuses as a cause of device failure. Check fuses before escalating.

    • ON/OFF switch stiff or unreliable

      Manual specifically lists switch stuck in ON or OFF position as a known failure mode. Functional for now but worth flagging in your notes as an early indicator.
