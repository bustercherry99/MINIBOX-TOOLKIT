Product Overview
What the MiniBox+ is, what it does, and why it matters
ⓘ
One-line pitch: The MiniBox+ is the only portable, cabinless pulmonary function testing device that delivers complete Spirometry, Lung Volumes (plethysmography), and DLCO — equivalent to the body box — in 15 to 20 minutes, operable by any medical office staff member, with no maintenance fees.
⚡ Fast
Complete PFT in as little as 15 to 20 minutes. Lung volumes in as little as 60 seconds of tidal breathing. Traditional body box takes 30 to 45 minutes.
✅ Easy
Fully automatic LVM and DLCO. 6 buttons from start to finish. Anyone on your medical office staff can operate it. No specialized RT required.
📈 Accurate
Clinically proven equivalent to body plethysmography in over 700 patients across 11 medical centers. Published in CHEST 2021. Same CPT codes as the body box.
👤 Comfortable
Cabinless — no claustrophobia, no anxiety. Tidal breathing only for LVM. Can test obese, wheelchair-bound, or bedridden patients. Ideal for pediatrics from age 5.
🔭 Portable
Smallest footprint of any plethysmography device. Sits on a desktop. Moves between exam rooms. Can be wheeled on optional dedicated cart with DLCO gas cylinder.
💰 No Maintenance
Zero maintenance fees. No parts contract. No monthly sensor cleaning. No annual PM required. Self-calibrating before every test. 5-minute wipe-down between patients.

What It Measures
Complete set of 30+ parameters, fully ATS/ERS compliant
🤏 Spirometry (FVC)
FEV1, FVC, FEV1/FVC, FEF 25-75, PEF, FEV6, MVV, SVC, and more. Pre and post bronchodilator. Methacholine challenge. Provocation testing. 6-Minute Walk Test.
🍧 Lung Volumes (LVM)
TLC, FRC, RV, VC, IC, ERV — same volumes as body plethysmography. Measures trapped air that gas dilution methods miss in obstructive disease. GLI reference values standard.
💥 Diffusing Capacity (DLCO)
DLCO, VA, DLCO/VA (KCO). Single-breath method. Fully automatic. Differentiates damage from COVID vs. COPD vs. asthma. Identifies ILD, emphysema, pulmonary vascular disease.

Patient Coaching Quick Reference
Setup, technique, and what to watch for during each test
ⓘ
Universal setup for LVM and DLCO: HHU attached to MiniBox+. Adjust device height for patient. Lips sealed tightly over mouthpiece, teeth over mouthpiece, nose clip applied.
FVC
Forced Vital Capacity
HHU detached from MiniBox+ for zeroing — then handed to patient. ATS requires 3 to 8 efforts.
Setup
  • 1
    Detach HHU from MiniBox+ and allow to calibrate (zeroing).
  • 2
    Hand HHU to patient. Seal lips around mouthpiece, teeth over it, apply nose clip.
Coaching the Patient
  • •
    Start with 2 to 3 normal relaxed breaths.
  • •
    Deep inhale, then immediate forced fast exhale.
  • •
    Squeeze ALL air out until the wheel turns GREEN.
  • •
    Deep breath back in to close the flow volume loop.
  • •
    Coach them to "BLAST" not just "blow." Prompt the sharp start — no hesitation at top of inhale.
LVM
Lung Volume Measurement
HHU attached to MiniBox+. Must be performed after FVC. Counter must reach SIX successful events.
Setup
  • 1
    Attach HHU to MiniBox+. Adjust device height for patient.
  • 2
    Patient seals lips and applies nose clip.
Coaching the Patient
  • •
    Relaxed normal breathing for about one minute. Keep breaths below 1 Liter.
  • •
    Once counter turns GREEN and reads "6," on the NEXT breath take a slow deep inhale to full capacity.
  • •
    Breathe out slow, squeeze all air out until the line hits plateau (empty).
  • •
    Take a breath in and come off mouthpiece.
  • •
    Watch for downward or upward sloping graph — indicates air leak.
DLCO
Diffusing Capacity
HHU attached. Minimum 2, maximum 5 tests. Allow 4 minutes between tests for gas clearance.
Setup
  • 1
    Attach HHU to MiniBox+. Adjust height. Patient seals lips, applies nose clip.
  • 2
    Start on an inhale and complete FOUR normal tidal breaths.
Coaching the Patient
  • •
    Patient exhales fully on the 4th breath.
  • •
    Wait for device to click (valve closes), then take a fast deep breath in to TLC.
  • •
    Inhalation must reach above the GREEN dotted line. Patient should inhale 85% of VC within 4 seconds.
  • •
    Hold breath for 8 to 10 seconds.
  • •
    Once wheel turns GREEN, exhale completely to empty. Then inhale and come off mouthpiece.
⚠
Common Failure Reasons & Fixes
FVC — "Don't hesitate, exhale faster"
Patient paused at the top of the inhalation (Evol exceeded 5% of FVC). Fix: coach them to immediately blast the air out the moment they reach peak inhalation — zero hesitation.
FVC — End-of-test criteria not found
Patient stopped exhaling before the plateau was reached. Fix: "Keep going, blow out every last bit of air." Common in COPD patients who have prolonged expiratory time.
LVM — TGV line not established
Tidal breathing was irregular before the maneuver. Fix: delete this test result (manual specifically recommends it). Allow patient to settle and breathe normally before restarting.
DLCO — Couldn't find stable parameters
The 4 required tidal breaths were skipped or unsteady. Fix: reinforce the sequence. Do NOT rush the patient into the maneuver. Wait the full 4 minutes before repeating.

Interpreting PFT Results
A stepwise clinical approach authored by Jeffrey Weiss, MD — Weiss Medical Allergy, Asthma & Immunology
ⓘ
The MiniBox+ automatically generates an ATS/ERS-based interpretation and decision tree with every report. The following is the clinical logic behind it. Physician review is always required.
1
Look at spirometry first. Normal or abnormal FEV1. If abnormal, look for other signs of an obstructive ventilatory defect: low FEF 25-75 and low FEV1/FVC.
2
If FVC is low, this could be a sign of a restrictive ventilatory defect. Check PEF to evaluate the effort of the maneuver.
3
If clinical history suggests asthma, administer a bronchodilator and check for a bronchodilator response (12% improvement in FEV1 or 20% improvement in FEF25-75). You can also perform methacholine challenge — a 15 to 20% decrease in FEV1 from the provocative dose is significant.
4
If FEV1 is normal but you're still concerned, check Lung Volumes next. If RV is elevated, this suggests air trapping — which could indicate an obstructive ventilatory defect (e.g. asthma).
5
If lung volumes show a decrease in TLC, this is indicative of a restrictive ventilatory defect.
6
If restriction is confirmed, check DLCO to determine the cause. Normal DLCO suggests extra-parenchymal processes (outside the lungs) such as a neuromuscular disorder. Low DLCO suggests an intra-parenchymal process (within the lungs) such as interstitial lung disease, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, sarcoidosis, or emphysema.
🌟
DLCO Clinical Pearls
Low DLCO — Think
Emphysema, Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD), Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis, Sarcoidosis, Pulmonary Vascular disease, Anemia, Early emphysema, Early ILD. Also present in COVID-related lung damage and COPD.
High DLCO — Think
Asthma, Obesity, Polycythemia, Intrapulmonary Hemorrhage. A high DLCO in context of obstruction often points toward asthma rather than emphysema.
Normal Spirometry + Low DLCO
May suggest Anemia, Pulmonary Vascular Disorders, Early ILD, or Early Emphysema. Lung volumes are normal but gas transfer is impaired — catch this early before spirometry changes.
Emphysema Pattern
Can present with airflow obstruction on PFTs AND low DLCO simultaneously. This combination is a key differentiator from asthma, where DLCO is typically normal or elevated.
💡
Why LVM matters for allergists and pulmonologists: Spirometry can appear normal even when air trapping is occurring. An elevated RV (residual volume) on the LVM test is the earliest indicator of obstructive disease — especially in asthma where the FEV1 may still be within normal range. This is a compelling reason to add LVM to every spirometry encounter.

Data Flow: What Goes In and What Comes Out
→ EMR to MiniBox (ORMO01 Order Message)
The EMR sends patient demographic and order information to the MiniBox before the test begins. This pre-populates the patient record automatically.
Required fields: Patient ID, Patient Name, Date of Birth, Sex, Message Type (ORM^O01), Universal Service ID (e.g., PFT13^COMPLETE PULMONARY FUNCTION TEST).
Valid test order codes:
PFT13^COMPLETE PULMONARY FUNCTION TEST → FVC + LVM + DLCO
PulmOneSPR^Pulm One Spirometry → FVC only
PulmOneSVC^Pulm One Slow Vital → SVC only
PulmOneMVV^Pulm One Voluntary → MVV only
PFT Full → FVC + LVM + DLCO
← MiniBox to EMR (ORUR01 Results Message)
After testing, the MiniBox automatically sends results back to the EMR. No manual data entry required.
What is transmitted per parameter: Best measured value, Predicted value, % Predicted, Lower Limit of Normal (LLN), Upper Limit of Normal (ULN), and Z-Score.
Also included: Full visit summary PDF report encoded in Base64 within the ORUR01 message — OR stored on a network drive with the filename embedded in the message. Pre and Post bronchodilator values sent as separate OBX segments.

Auto Logoff

CO and CH₄ Concentration Low
Work through each step in order before escalating to a repair
Step 1 — Verify Gas Mixture
Confirm the tank contains the correct mixture:

• 0.3% Methane (CH₄)
• 0.3% Carbon Monoxide (CO)
• 21% Oxygen (O₂)
• Balanced Nitrogen (N₂)
• 2% Accuracy Medical Grade
Step 2 — Check Tank Pressure & Volume
Verify both gauges on the regulator:

• Left gauge (Pressure): 70–75 PSI
• Right gauge (Volume): Above 500 PSI

If either reading is out of range, replace or refill the tank before proceeding.
Step 3 — Purge / Bleed the Gas Line
1. Turn off the gas tank completely.
2. Use the white knob to decrease pressure as far as it will go.
3. Disconnect the gas tubing from the regulator: press up the tiny black O-ring, then pull the tubing downward.
Step 4 — Cubic App Recalibration (Pre-2023 Devices Only)
If steps 1–3 don't resolve and device is older than 2023, use Cubic's app to recalibrate the gas analyzer. Watch the Cubic app training video first.

If the app still shows low CO and CH₄: device must come in for repair.

Common Test Failure Reasons & Fixes
Patient coaching corrections for FVC, LVM, and DLCO failures
FVC — Don't hesitate, exhale faster
Patient paused at the top of inhalation (Evol exceeded 5% of FVC). Fix: coach them to immediately blast air out the moment they reach peak inhalation — zero hesitation.
FVC — End-of-test criteria not found
Patient stopped exhaling before plateau was reached. Fix: "Keep going, blow out every last bit of air." Common in COPD patients with prolonged expiratory time.
LVM — TGV line not established
Tidal breathing was irregular before the maneuver. Fix: delete this test result (manual specifically recommends it). Let patient settle, then restart.
DLCO — Couldn't find stable parameters
The 4 required tidal breaths were skipped or unsteady. Fix: reinforce the full sequence. Do NOT rush the patient. Wait the full 4 minutes before repeating.

Component-by-Component Failure Modes
Eight headline failure modes in an aging MiniBox+ — each card shows bedside symptoms, clinical / QC impact, and urgency
#1
Fast-Response Valve / Occluder (LVM)
Manual spec: airway occlusion < 0.1 sec. A stuck or slow valve corrupts TGV and every lung volume derived from it.
RT Symptoms
  • •
    Audible delay or "soft" click when the shutter closes
  • •
    Repeat "TGV line not established" even with calm, well-coached tidal breathing
  • •
    LVM biologic control trending lower than the established baseline
  • •
    Mouth-pressure curve doesn't plateau cleanly against the closed shutter
Clinical / QC Impact
  • •
    TGV is underestimated — the patient keeps exhaling before the shutter seals, so mouth pressure builds against a leaking closure
  • •
    TLC, RV, and FRC all pulled down proportionally — restriction can be missed entirely
  • •
    Data passes device QC but is clinically wrong — the most dangerous failure mode
Urgency: HIGH. Per PulmOne manual §15.11, "Valve stuck in closed/open position" is an explicit replacement condition — action: "Contact PulmOne support to replace the device." Valve timing cannot be recalibrated in the field. And the silent-drift version of this failure throws no error code at all.
#2
DLCO Gas Analyzer (IR CO / CH₄ Cell)
Manual spec: CO ±1% FS, CH₄ ±2.5% FS, neutral point drift <2%/week, response time t₀‑₅₀ <85 ms. Aging IR cell loses sensitivity and drifts beyond these tolerances.
RT Symptoms
  • •
    DLCO trending lower than expected against patient history
  • •
    Repeat "Couldn't find stable parameters" flags
  • •
    CO / CH₄ concentration-low warnings despite a fresh, correctly-mixed tank
  • •
    Frequent need for Cubic-app recalibration (pre-2023 devices)
  • •
    EC117 errors that reappear after restart
Clinical / QC Impact
  • •
    DLCO under-read — ILD / emphysema progression can be masked or exaggerated
  • •
    Reproducibility outside ATS ±3 mL/min/mmHg → study cannot be billed as 94729
  • •
    Rework cascades — patients brought back, revenue lost per visit
Urgency: HIGH. PulmOne manual §15.9 requires a weekly DLCO calibration check (3-L syringe, target DLCO 0 ± 1 mL/min/mmHg). Per the manual: if the check has "failed multiple times despite being performed thoroughly, contact your PulmOne representative." Field recalibration buys weeks, not years. Once the IR cell is end-of-life, DLCO drift accelerates.
#3
Main Board / Motherboard
Controller electronics aging — watchdog resets, freezes, lost tests.
RT Symptoms
  • •
    EC102G HUB board errors that return after reboot
  • •
    Main-board watchdog errors (hardware failure — no remote fix)
  • •
    Random freezes mid-DLCO or mid-LVM
  • •
    Boot loops / device refuses to power up cleanly
  • •
    Lost patient test data mid-session
Clinical / QC Impact
  • •
    Patients rescheduled — direct revenue and throughput hit
  • •
    Clinician confidence erodes as tests "keep failing"
  • •
    Partial test records create documentation gaps that complicate billing
Urgency: HIGH. Main-board failure is the common end-of-life event. Once watchdog resets appear, full failure typically follows within weeks to months — and device must come in for repair, taking the lab offline.
#4
Sensor Board / HHU (Pressure & Flow Transducers)
Transducer drift in the handheld unit — every maneuver is affected.
RT Symptoms
  • •
    Sensor-board watchdog error (hardware failure)
  • •
    EC11H — HHU not communicating reliably
  • •
    FVC values drift on biologic control even after fresh flow calibration
  • •
    Unit won't zero-calibrate, or zero drifts during a session
Clinical / QC Impact
  • •
    FVC / FEV₁ drift → false GOLD staging for COPD patients
  • •
    Obstruction severity mis-classified
  • •
    Spirometry may fall outside ATS/ERS acceptability
Urgency: HIGH. HHU may be replaced independently of the main device, but recurring sensor drift on an aging unit often signals broader electronics aging.
#5
Flow Sensor / Pneumotach (Handheld Unit)
Manual spec: volume ±2.5% or 50 mL, flow 0–14 L/sec, resistance <1.2 cmH₂O/L/sec at 12 L/sec. ATS-required daily cal check — repeated failures signal wear.
RT Symptoms
  • •
    3-L syringe calibration consistently out of tolerance
  • •
    High-flow (PEFR) repeatability degrading
  • •
    Low-flow end-of-test plateau hard to capture even with cooperative patients
Clinical / QC Impact
  • •
    Spirometry outside ATS accuracy bounds → repeat tests
  • •
    PEFR unreliable for asthma monitoring
  • •
    Billing at risk if QC fails on audit review
Urgency: HIGH. Per manual §15.11, "Flow sensor fault" is another explicit replacement condition — action: "Contact PulmOne support to replace the device." Chronic syringe-check failures in an aging unit mean the flow path is at end-of-life, and the manufacturer themselves say replace, not repair.
#6
Internal Sample Pump
Diaphragm / motor wear — sample flow degrades, DLCO sampling suffers.
RT Symptoms
  • •
    Test hangs longer on the "sampling" phase than it used to
  • •
    Audible change in pump tone — louder or uneven
  • •
    Intermittent DLCO sample-volume flags
Clinical / QC Impact
  • •
    Incomplete gas sampling → DLCO reproducibility fails ATS
  • •
    Cascades with gas analyzer aging — the two compound
Urgency: MEDIUM. Pump-only repair possible, but usually co-occurs with other pneumatic aging.
#7
Pneumatic Tubing, Solenoid Valves & Internal Micro-Leaks
Decade-old silicone lines and solenoids develop pinhole leaks and slow switching.
RT Symptoms
  • •
    Gas tank volume dropping faster than historical usage
  • •
    "Low gas concentration" warnings even after Steps 1–3 of the CO/CH₄ troubleshooting flow
  • •
    BTPS / leak flags on DLCO attempts
  • •
    Solenoid switching sounds sluggish or delayed
Clinical / QC Impact
  • •
    Inspired gas dilution → DLCO is underestimated
  • •
    Obstruction can be masked on DLCO-only workups
  • •
    Slow valves push maneuvers outside standardized timing windows
Urgency: MEDIUM. Often the first thing to quietly fail and the hardest to diagnose without a teardown.
#8
Power Supply & Aging Capacitors
Electrolytic capacitors dry out over ~10 years — the silent predictor of total failure.
RT Symptoms
  • •
    Intermittent reboots with no obvious trigger
  • •
    Longer boot time than when the unit was new
  • •
    Fan noise increasing; unit warm to the touch
  • •
    Watchdog events occurring overnight / after idle periods
Clinical / QC Impact
  • •
    Lost test data mid-session
  • •
    Cascading stress on main board, sensor board, and pumps (marginal voltage accelerates downstream failures)
  • •
    Eventual total failure — usually without warning
Urgency: MEDIUM, trending HIGH. Rarely the presenting complaint, but once capacitor aging is contributing to watchdog events, total failure is a matter of "when," not "if."
⚙
Secondary & Supporting Failure Modes
Smaller failures that compound the headline issues — each one is another reason to document
Connectors & Cables (HHU, CU Port, USB)
Symptoms: EC11H, EC11M errors; intermittent HHU disconnects; cable wiggles bring the device back.

Impact: Every aborted test is 15–20 min of RT time plus a patient visit at risk. Connector fatigue on a decade-old unit cascades fast.
Environmental Sensors (T / RH / Barometric)
Symptoms: BTPS correction drifts away from ambient conditions; lung volumes off even with a clean mechanical calibration.

Impact: All volumes corrected through BTPS are affected — spirometry and lung volumes both.
Calibration & Biologic Control Drift
Symptoms: BioQC chart trending outside ±5%; daily cal more frequent than weekly protocol requires.

Impact: Meta-signal that something upstream is failing. If BioQC has drifted, document the trend line — it's audit-grade evidence.
Tablet / HHU Display & Touch
Symptoms: Unresponsive touchscreen mid-test; display dimming or backlight flicker.

Impact: Tests aborted when RT can't tap "accept." Patient frustration, RT workarounds become unsafe.
Internal Fan / Thermal Management
Symptoms: Fan whine, unit warm to the touch, thermal throttling events, shutdowns after long DLCO sessions.

Impact: Accelerates capacitor and IR-cell aging. Hot runs wear everything else faster.
Filter Line Saturation & Moisture
Symptoms: Back-pressure creep; gas concentration low even with fresh filters; moisture in the sample line.

Impact: On an aging unit, moisture ingress accelerates gas analyzer end-of-life and pump wear.

The IDN Case — Why Waiting Costs More
Translate the bedside symptoms above into the language leadership funds
Patient Safety & Clinical Accuracy
An aging fast occluder or IR cell will not throw an error when its accuracy degrades. Patients can be mis-staged for COPD, ILD, or pulmonary hypertension based on silently-wrong numbers. Every delayed replacement widens this risk window.
Revenue Loss From Rework
A complete PFT (94010 + 94726 + 94729) reimburses ~$151.45 on national Medicare averages. Every repeat study driven by device drift is a revenue forgone — and patient reschedules pull one visit into two.
Downtime = Revenue Gap
Once the unit has to ship for repair, the PFT lab is dark until it returns. At 25 complete PFTs/week, every week of downtime ≈ $3,786 in missed reimbursement, not counting referral leakage to outside labs.
Regulatory & Audit Risk
Tests that fall outside ATS/ERS acceptability standards cannot be billed. Persistent QC drift creates audit exposure — and biologic control charts are the first thing a reviewer asks for.
Ordering Clinician Confidence
When pulmonologists start second-guessing numbers, they stop ordering. Referrals migrate. Rebuilding that trust after a preventable failure takes far longer than a proactive replacement cycle.
RT Time & Burnout
Every rework is 15–20 minutes of RT time that could be spent on another patient. Aging equipment quietly taxes staffing. Replacement restores throughput — and morale.
💡
The "wait for it to die" calculation is usually wrong. When you add up silent-inaccuracy risk + rework revenue loss + weeks of downtime when the unit finally fails + audit exposure, the total cost of waiting exceeds the cost of a planned, scheduled replacement almost every time.
