Screening vs. monitoring — know the difference before you count revenue
Medicare policy states plainly that coverage “excludes routine (screening) tests for asymptomatic patients with or without high risk of lung disease.” The ACR/CHEST guideline recommends screening at-risk patients; Medicare does not pay for it. Both are true, and no policy reconciles them.
The line that works: a patient with documented lung involvement or respiratory symptoms is being monitored — that is diagnostic and payable. A truly asymptomatic patient with no established lung disease is being screened — that is patient-liable and needs an ABN.
Count Section 1 honestly: only patients who clear the monitoring bar belong in a revenue projection. Coding a symptom a patient does not have, to force payment, is a False Claims Act problem.

Drug Monitoring — How to Code It
— Patient is on a high-risk drug and you're concerned. Find your scenario, use those codes, add your count.

The drug alone doesn't generate the billing code — but the combination of their diagnosis, the drug they're on, and your documented clinical concern does. Match the scenario below to the situation.

ⓘ
Finding your count: Run a medication report in your EMR — search by drug name (methotrexate, cyclophosphamide, leflunomide, etc.) to pull every active patient on that drug. Most systems can do this in under two minutes. That number goes in the tally box below each scenario.
Scenario 1
Routine monitoring — no symptoms

Patient is on a high-risk drug (MTX, CYC, leflunomide, JAK inhibitor). No complaints. You want a baseline or periodic check.

Underlying Dx Z79.631 Z79.630 Z79.622 Z79.620

Use the drug-specific code — not Z79.899. Methotrexate Z79.631 · cyclophosphamide Z79.630 · JAK inhibitor Z79.622 · immunosuppressive biologic Z79.620 · azathioprine or MMF Z79.624. Z79.899 is correct only where no specific code exists — leflunomide and hydroxychloroquine.

The weakest of the three — say so honestly. A status code with no symptom and no established lung disease is a thin medical-necessity story. Routine PFT surveillance of the asymptomatic methotrexate patient is not supported by the evidence base, and the 2021 ACR RA guideline makes no such monitoring recommendation. Baseline testing in a patient with symptoms or known lung disease is defensible; a standing test-everyone protocol is not.

94010 Spirometry (FVC, FEV1)  ·  94726 Plethysmography / lung volumes (or 94727 if your lab uses gas dilution / washout)  ·  94729 DLCO / diffusing capacity — add-on only, must accompany 94010 or 94726/94727

Not 94060 for ILD. 94060 is spirometry with bronchodilator — a reversible-obstruction test. NCCI pairs it with 94010 at modifier indicator 0, meaning no modifier will ever unbundle them; billing both is a hard denial. The ACR/CHEST ILD guideline never mentions bronchodilator testing. Two further consequences worth knowing: 94060 carries direct supervision (a physician must be in the office suite), while 94010, 94726, 94727 and 94729 need only general supervision — so the correct ILD panel is also the easier one to staff.

Z79.631 Methotrexate  ·  Z79.630 Cyclophosphamide  ·  Z79.622 JAK inhibitor  ·  Z79.620 Immunosuppressive biologic  ·  Z79.624 Azathioprine / MMF  ·  Z79.899 only where no specific code exists (leflunomide, hydroxychloroquine)  ·  R06.00 Dyspnea  ·  R05.3 Chronic cough  ·  T45.1X5A Adverse effect of immunosuppressive drugs  ·  J70.2 Acute / J70.3 Chronic drug-induced interstitial pneumonitis

The Z79.6– drug-class family replaced Z79.899 for these agents effective 1 October 2022. If a claim still carries Z79.899 for methotrexate, it is three years out of date. When the drug has actually caused lung injury, sequence the manifestation first (J70.2 / J70.3) and the adverse-effect code second (T45.1X5A).

Reimbursement estimates based on Medicare national averages (CY2026 national unadjusted: 94010 $29.73 + 94726 $63.80 + 94729 $63.46 = $156.99). Rates vary by geography, MAC, payer, and year — verify current rates before billing. Medicare reimburses based on documented medical necessity; there is no national frequency cap on PFTs, but repeat testing must be justified by documented clinical change, and routine testing at every office visit is expressly not reimbursable. Note that the standalone Medicare PFT coverage policies (LCD L34247 / article A57216) were retired in November 2023 and folded into the Respiratory Care policy — frequency figures still circulating from those documents are obsolete. Several MAC jurisdictions, including New York, now have no PFT-specific policy at all, which means no covered-diagnosis list to fail and no safe harbour either. Every study requires a signed written interpretation; a computer-generated report needs a physician signature attesting to review and accuracy. Coverage excludes routine screening of asymptomatic patients.
