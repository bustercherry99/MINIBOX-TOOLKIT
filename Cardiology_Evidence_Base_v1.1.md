## Use case 1 — Dyspnea differentiation (cardiac vs pulmonary)

**Verified claim:** In the evaluation of chronic dyspnea, spirometry is a recommended first-line diagnostic test (alongside CBC, basic chemistry, ECG, chest radiography, and pulse oximetry). Cardiac and pulmonary disease are the likeliest causes of dyspnea, and spirometry should be performed when a pulmonary cause is suspected.

**Source:** Syed Z. "Chronic Dyspnea: Diagnosis and Evaluation." *American Family Physician.* 2020;101(9):542–548.
https://www.aafp.org/pubs/afp/issues/2020/0501/p542.html

**Strength:** High — current, physician-facing, guideline-style review. Directly supports "spirometry belongs in the first-line dyspnea workup."

**How we use it:** Positions the MiniBox as the tool that completes the first-line workup in-office instead of deferring the pulmonary half to an outside referral.

## Use case 2 — Heart failure vs COPD overlap

**Verified claim:** COPD is common and substantially underdiagnosed in heart failure patients. When spirometry is used as the diagnostic tool, COPD prevalence among HF patients is reported in the range of roughly 25–50%. Airflow obstruction in stable HF is frequent and severely underdiagnosed; spirometry should be considered in HF patients to improve diagnosis and treatment of concomitant pulmonary disease.

**Sources:**
- Minasian AG, et al. "Prevalence of airflow obstruction in patients with stable systolic heart failure." (PMC5219786) — reports obstructive pattern in ~39% of stable HF patients; concludes airflow obstruction is frequent and severely underdiagnosed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5219786/
- "The Complex Relationship Between Heart Failure and Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease: A Comprehensive Review." (PMC12250576) — states COPD prevalence among HF patients is 25–50% when spirometry is the diagnostic tool. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12250576/

**Caveat to respect in copy:** Pulmonary congestion in HF can itself cause a reversible obstructive pattern that improves with diuresis. So "obstruction on spirometry" ≠ automatic COPD. Framing should be "spirometry helps separate and co-manage overlapping disease," not "spirometry diagnoses COPD in HF patients." This nuance is exactly what a cardiologist will check us on.

**Strength:** High for the "common and underdiagnosed" claim. The 25–50% figure is well-supported but is a range from the literature, not a single guideline number — cite it as a range.

## Use case 3 — Pre-op / peri-procedural risk assessment

**Verified claim:** Abnormal pulmonary function is associated with postoperative pulmonary complications (e.g., respiratory failure, pneumonia, atelectasis) after cardiac surgery. Spirometry helps identify obstructive or restrictive defects; an FEV₁/FVC ratio below 70% has been described as an independent risk factor for postoperative pulmonary complications.

**Sources:**
- "Pulmonary function testing in preoperative high-risk patients." *Perioperative Medicine* (2024). (PMC10913451) https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10913451/
- "Spirometry Assessment and Correlation With Postoperative Pulmonary Complications in Cardiac Surgery Patients." (PMC7682508) https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7682508/

**Caveat to respect in copy:** Spirometry's sensitivity/specificity for predicting postoperative complications is imperfect (reported sensitivities ~38–84%, specificities ~55–99%). It is a risk-stratification aid, not a pass/fail gate. Do not overstate predictive power.

**Strength:** Moderate–high for "abnormal PFTs associate with worse post-op pulmonary outcomes." Position as risk stratification, not guarantee.

## Use case 4 — Cardiotoxic drug monitoring & pulmonary hypertension

**Verified claim:** The 2022 ESC Cardio-Oncology guidelines address detection and monitoring of pulmonary hypertension linked to certain cancer therapies. Dasatinib (a tyrosine kinase inhibitor) is strongly associated with pulmonary hypertension; when tricuspid regurgitant velocity exceeds 3.4 m/s, dasatinib should be withheld and the diagnosis confirmed by right heart catheterization.

**Sources:**
- Lyon AR, et al. "2022 ESC Guidelines on cardio-oncology." *European Heart Journal.* 2022;43(41):4229–4361. https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/43/41/4229/6673995
- ACC key-points summary: "2022 ESC Guidelines on Cardio-Oncology: Key Points." https://www.acc.org/Latest-in-Cardiology/ten-points-to-remember/2022/08/29/00/01/2022-ESC-Guidelines-on-Cardio-Oncology-ESC-2022

**Caveat to respect in copy:** The ESC guideline's primary monitoring tools for cardiotoxicity are echocardiography and biomarkers; PH surveillance is drug-specific (notably dasatinib). This use case is narrower than the other three — frame it as a targeted surveillance role (drug-induced PH / pulmonary toxicity monitoring), not routine PFT screening for all oncology patients. This is the weakest of the four hooks for a general cardiology audience and may be better positioned as a specialty/sub-segment angle.

**Strength:** Moderate. Accurate but narrow. Consider leading with use cases 1–3 for a general cardiology practice.

## Use case 5 — Why a FULL PFT, not just spirometry (the DLCO argument)

**Why this section exists:** The MiniBox does a *complete* PFT — spirometry **+ lung volumes (plethysmography) + DLCO (diffusing capacity)**. A plain spirometer does not measure DLCO. For a cardiologist, DLCO is the single most valuable of the three, because it reflects the gas-exchange surface and pulmonary vasculature — exactly where heart disease shows up in the lungs.

**Verified claims:**
- DLCO is a recognized diagnostic and prognostic marker in pulmonary vascular disease and pulmonary hypertension, ranking among the important prognostic parameters alongside 6-minute walk distance, age, and NT-proBNP.
- In patients with pulmonary hypertension due to **heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF)**, a DLCO below 45% of predicted was found in nearly half of patients, and reduced DLCO is associated with higher mortality.
- A low FVC/DLCO ratio helps flag pulmonary hypertension (e.g., in connective tissue disease and COPD-associated PH).

**Sources:**
- "The prognostic value of DLCO and pulmonary blood flow in patients with pulmonary hypertension." (PMC6935895) https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6935895/
- "Diffusion Capacity and Mortality in Patients With Pulmonary Hypertension Due to Heart Failure With Preserved Ejection Fraction." *JACC: Heart Failure.* 2016. https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jchf.2015.12.016
- "Diagnostic value of combined FVC%/DLCO% and echocardiography in CTD-associated pulmonary hypertension." (PMC10811443) https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10811443/

**Strength:** High for "DLCO carries diagnostic and prognostic value in PH and HFpEF." This is the strongest, most cardiology-specific reason to prefer a complete PFT over spirometry alone — and one source is a cardiology journal (JACC: Heart Failure). Lead the "why full PFT" story with the HFpEF/DLCO finding.
