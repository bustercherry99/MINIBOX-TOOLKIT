Pre-Call Prep & Discovery
Every great demo starts 48 hours before you walk in the door
ⓘ
The rule: a MiniBox+ demo without prep is a 50% conversation. With 30 minutes of research, the same demo becomes 90% relevant — and physicians notice the difference instantly.
📋
Account Research Checklist
Knock these out before you step into the practice — tap to mark complete
Practice size: how many providers, how many physical locations, PCP vs specialty split.
Current PFT setup: do they own a body box, a spirometer only, or send patients out? (NPI database + Doximity + website billing page)
Patient mix: COPD heavy? Allergy/asthma? Post-COVID long-haul? Pediatric? Pre-op clearance? (Review their website, provider bios, patient-review sites)
Volume estimate: multiply providers x 3-5 PFT-eligible patients per day x working days. This becomes your ROI anchor.
Payer mix: Medicare-heavy? Commercial? Any known contract issues with 94726/94729? (CMS data + State Medical Board + NCQA)
Decision-maker map: who owns the device purchase — owning physician, managing partner, practice administrator, CFO of a group? Never walk in blind.
Existing equipment age: if they have a body box, is it Jaeger, MGC, Morgan? If ultrasound, is it NDD? Drive the replace-vs-complement framing early.
EMR system: Epic, Athena, Cerner, NextGen, eClinicalWorks? Pre-empt the "does it integrate?" objection.
Competitive footprint: has an NDD / Jaeger / MGC rep been in recently? LinkedIn activity, recent posts, conference appearances.
Personal hook: alma mater, fellowship, publications, board positions, hobbies. One human detail doubles rapport in the first 60 seconds.

Discovery Questions — By Role
Ask different questions of different stakeholders. Never lead with price.
The Physician
Clinical
"When you see a new COPD or asthma patient, what PFTs do you run before you finalize the treatment plan?"
Listening for: spirometry only vs full PFT. If spirometry only, they're under-diagnosing — open the "trapped air" conversation.
Pain
"What happens when you need lung volumes and DLCO — do you send them out, schedule them back, or skip the test?"
Listening for: workflow friction. 40% of referred PFTs never happen. That's your opening.
Emotion
"How often do you get a PFT report back from the hospital and wish you could just review it in front of the patient?"
Listening for: frustration. Anchor to same-visit results: patient leaves with a diagnosis, not another appointment.
Future
"If you could run complete PFTs in your own exam rooms in 15 minutes, what would change for your practice?"
Listening for: their vision. Make them say it. Whatever they describe is your closing hook.
The Practice Manager / Admin
Operational
"Where do you currently lose time — scheduling, rescheduling, or re-running failed tests?"
Listening for: 19% test failure rate on body boxes (Verona study). MiniBox+ is 8.2%. That's hours back per week.
Financial
"What's your annual maintenance spend on existing equipment — PM contracts, sensor replacement, consumables?"
Listening for: $4K-6K in PM contracts. MiniBox+ is $0.
Billing
"Are you billing 94726 today? What percent of those get reimbursed on first submission?"
Listening for: denial history. MiniBox+ bills same codes as body box; easy to transfer workflow.
Staffing
"Who runs your PFTs today — a dedicated RT, a cross-trained MA, or the doctor?"
Listening for: staffing cost. MiniBox+ can be run by any MA after 2 hours of training. Frees the RT or unblocks a practice that has none.
The Owning Partner / CFO
Revenue
"What's the gap between how many PFTs you should be running clinically and how many you actually run each month?"
Listening for: the uncaptured-revenue number. Multiply by $151 and you have the case in one breath.
Capital
"How do you typically finance equipment — capital, operating lease, or Section 179 tax-advantaged purchase?"
Listening for: financing preference. Section 179 deduction + no-interest plans are often the unlock.
Strategic
"Are you planning to expand service lines or add satellite locations in the next 18 months?"
Listening for: growth. MiniBox+ moves between exam rooms and locations. One unit, multiple sites.
Decision
"If the numbers work, what's your process to get from yes to purchase order?"
Listening for: signature chain and blockers. Surface these in meeting one, not meeting three.

The Non-Negotiables
Walk in knowing these 3 things or reschedule
1. Decision-maker confirmed
The person with signature authority must be in the room, or at minimum have blocked 15 minutes on the calendar to join. If you're demoing to the MA, you're rehearsing — not selling.
2. Volume anchor set
Before you walk in, you must have a defensible patient-volume number in your head. "I'll figure it out in the meeting" costs you the ROI story.
3. Specific objection anticipated
Know their likely primary objection before they say it. "They already have a body box" or "they already quoted NDD" — plan the first 90 seconds of your response.

In-Office Demo Playbook
Three scripted flows — 15-minute hallway, 30-minute exam-room, 60-minute lunch-and-learn
💡
The golden rule of a MiniBox+ demo: run the device on a real person within the first 7 minutes. Nothing else you say matters as much as the physician watching a 60-second tidal-breathing LVM complete in front of them.
⏰
The 15-Minute Hallway Demo
When you catch the doctor between patients — momentum over completeness
00:00 – 01:30
The Hook
One sentence, one question, one permission ask
What You Do
  • Open device, power on, hand MA the HHU
  • State the one-line pitch
  • Ask a closed-ended yes/no question
What You Say
"Doctor, this is the MiniBox+. Complete PFT — spirometry, lung volumes, DLCO — in 15 minutes, runs on your desk, no maintenance fees. Would you be willing to give me 14 more minutes to show you it work on a real patient?"
01:30 – 09:00
The Live Test
FVC + LVM + DLCO on a volunteer (MA, front-desk, you)
What You Do
  • Run FVC first — 90 seconds
  • Run LVM (plethysmography, tidal breathing) — 60 seconds
  • Run DLCO — 90 seconds
  • Print report, hand to physician
What You Say During
"Watch how few buttons this takes. Watch the LVM — tidal breathing only, no panting. That's the piece that makes it runnable by any staff member. Sixty seconds of breathing and we have TLC."
09:00 – 12:00
The Revenue Moment
Show the ROI page of the Master Toolkit on the iPad
What You Do
  • Open Master Toolkit → CPT & Revenue tab
  • Dial to their estimated weekly volume
  • Tap "Present to Physician" for fullscreen mode
  • Let the number land in silence
What You Say
"At just 5 complete PFTs a day — which is conservative for a practice your size — this device pays for itself in about 6 working weeks. After that, it's pure revenue to your practice."
12:00 – 15:00
The Commitment Ask
Leave-behind delivered, next meeting booked
What You Do
  • Email the 1-page physician summary to them right now
  • Ask for a 30-minute follow-up with the practice manager
  • Leave quote or proposal on the desk, not in email
What You Say
"I just emailed you a one-page summary you can share with your partner. What does Thursday look like for a 30-minute sit-down with whoever runs your billing — so we can walk through your specific numbers?"

The 30-Minute Exam-Room Demo
Your default flow — enough time for objection handling and commitment
00:00 – 03:00
Rapport & Problem Framing
Ask 1 Pre-Call discovery question. Listen. Mirror.
What You Do
  • Reference the hook you dug up (fellowship, publication, patient population)
  • Ask one open-ended clinical question
  • Shut up. Let them talk for 2 minutes.
What You Say
"Before I show you anything — I read you did your fellowship at Hopkins. When you see a new COPD patient today, what PFTs do you typically run before you finalize the treatment plan?"
03:00 – 06:00
The Setup Frame
Position MiniBox+ before you demo it — context is everything
What You Do
  • State the one-line pitch
  • Name the 3 things that make it different (speed, any-staff, zero maintenance)
  • Call out the CHEST 2021 validation — 700+ pts, 11 centers
What You Say
"The reason I came out personally is this device is the only one validated against the body box in a peer-reviewed study that doesn't require a PFT lab, a dedicated RT, or a $5K PM contract. I want to show you it run — and then walk you through what happens to your revenue."
06:00 – 16:00
The Live Test on Two Volunteers
One healthy, one with known obstructive or restrictive pattern if possible
What You Do
  • First patient: healthy control, full PFT, < 8 min
  • Second patient: obese, elderly, wheelchair, or pediatric — show the range
  • Print both reports; walk through interpretation
  • Call out the 6-button simplicity throughout
What You Say
"Watch the workflow — six buttons from start to report. No panting, no claustrophobic chamber. You can test a patient who can't tolerate a body box. And the report you're holding is the same set of parameters you'd get from a hospital PFT lab."
16:00 – 22:00
The Revenue Walkthrough
Use the ROI calculator fullscreen mode on iPad
What You Do
  • Set volume to THEIR estimated number, not a generic 25
  • Show breakeven date — they understand dates better than payback months
  • Walk 5-year cumulative bar chart left to right slowly
  • Mention Section 179 — "I'll put you in touch with your accountant"
What You Say
"At your patient volume, your breakeven is [specific date]. After that, every PFT you run is revenue that used to go out the door to the hospital. In year 5 you've banked [specific number]."
22:00 – 26:00
Handle the First Objection

"That's the right question to ask. Here's what the numbers say — [scripted response]. Does that change how you're thinking about it?"
26:00 – 30:00
Trial Close & Next Step

What You Do

The 60-Minute Lunch-and-Learn
For the whole practice — physician, admin, billing, MAs — at once
00 – 10
Room Intros & Clinical Framing
You don't own the conversation — you catalyze it
What You Do
  • Have every person in the room say their role and their #1 frustration with PFTs today
  • Take notes visibly — they see you listening
  • Reflect back the themes: "So it sounds like the three things are X, Y, and Z."
What You Say
"Before I demo anything, I want to hear from each of you what you wish was different about how PFTs get done here. I promise I'll connect every feature I show back to what you just told me."
10 – 30
Live Testing — Multiple Volunteers
MA runs the device, not you. This is the magic moment.
What You Do
  • Spend 5 minutes training the MA on the 6-button workflow
  • Let the MA run a full PFT on a volunteer with you narrating
  • Physician watches their own staff run the test unassisted
  • "They just learned this in 5 minutes. Imagine what 2 hours of training looks like."
What You Say
"Sarah, I want you to run this one. I'll coach. Just press the button labeled 'New Patient'... perfect. Follow the prompts. And in a few minutes, the doctor's going to see you produce a full PFT report — the same thing a hospital RT produces in 45 minutes."
30 – 45
Financial Deep-Dive
Practice manager + billing lead in the seat
What You Do
  • Open the PulmOne ROI Proposal Builder, customize to their practice
  • Show CPT codes side by side with their existing billing
  • Discuss Section 179, no-interest financing, lease vs buy
  • Print or email the customized proposal on the spot
What You Say
"I'm going to build a proposal with your numbers in it right now. You'll have a PDF you can walk into a partner meeting with before I leave."
45 – 55
Q&A + Objection Round
Go in order: clinical, operational, financial, timing
What You Do
  • Invite all questions openly
  • Never answer two at once — take them one at a time
  • Watch for group dynamics: who's on your side, who's skeptical
  • Thank the skeptic first when they push back
What You Say
"Great question — that was the second thing I thought about too. Here's what the data says..."
55 – 60
Three-Way Commitment Close
Physician, admin, billing — each commits to one next step
What You Do
  • Doctor: agrees to sponsor the decision to the partner
  • Admin: agrees to pull the billing data for a real pro-forma
  • Billing: agrees to verify 94726 reimbursement history
  • Schedule follow-up before you leave the room
What You Say
"Here's what I'd like to walk out with: Doctor — you take this to your partner. Linda — you pull the last 12 months of PFT-eligible encounters. Jose — you confirm 94726 has been paying in your top three payers. We reconvene next Thursday. Are we all in?"

Objection Library
30 common objections, each with the scripted response and supporting proof point

CLINICAL
Clinical & Accuracy Objections
Objection
"I don't trust any lung volume measurement that isn't a body box."
Response
"That's exactly how every pulmonologist I respect feels. That's why the MiniBox+ is the only device validated head-to-head against body plethysmography in a peer-reviewed, multi-center, 700+ patient study — published in CHEST in 2021. In 10 of 14 cases where the results diverged, the MiniBox+ was actually more clinically accurate than the body box. This isn't gas dilution or estimation — it's true plethysmography, just without the cabin."
Proof: Berger K, Adam O, Kaminsky D, et al. CHEST Feb 2021. 266 pts across 5 global centers.
Objection
"DLCO in 15 minutes seems too fast — how accurate can it be?"
Response
"Fair. Here's why it's fast — it's fully automated. The patient takes 4 tidal breaths, exhales, inhales to TLC, holds 8-10 seconds, exhales. Single-breath method, same protocol as the body box. Time savings come from automation, not skipping steps. You still wait 4 minutes between repeat tests for gas clearance."
Proof: ATS/ERS 2017 single-breath DLCO standard; MiniBox+ test time 7.2 min vs body box 13.9 min (Verona 2021).
Objection
"What about patients with severe obstruction — will this really measure their trapped air?"
Response
"That's exactly where MiniBox+ beats N₂ washout and helium dilution. Gas dilution methods systematically underestimate TLC in severe COPD because gas can't reach air trapped behind narrowed airways. MiniBox+ is true plethysmography — it measures all gas in the thorax, including what's trapped. Truer disease picture, and it bills the higher CPT 94726 instead of the lower 94727."
Proof: ATS/ERS technical standard; published NSD comparison table (MiniBox+ 8.9% vs helium dilution 15.3%).
Objection
"My pediatric patients won't cooperate — FVC in kids is hard enough."
Response
"That's why MiniBox+ is a better pediatric tool than a body box. No claustrophobic chamber, no scary panting maneuver. LVM is tidal breathing only — kids already do that naturally. We've tested children from age 5 successfully. And the whole thing takes 15 minutes instead of 45 — huge for attention span."
Proof: LVM requires only tidal breathing per validated protocol; published pediatric use cases on file.
Objection
"My obese patients can't fit in a body box — does this actually help them?"
Response
"MiniBox+ has no chamber at all. We've tested wheelchair-bound, bedridden, and obese patients with no adaptation required. The patient stands or sits in a normal chair, holds the handheld unit. That's one of the reasons this device opens up PFT access to patient populations the body box literally cannot accommodate."
Proof: Cabinless design documented; HHU-only patient interface.

STALL
Stalls, Delays, Political Objections
Objection
"Let me think about it."
Response
"Of course — this is a real decision. Let me ask: when you say think about it, what are the specific questions still running through your head? Is it the money, the workflow, the clinical fit, or the timing? If I know which one it is, I can get you the answer tonight instead of in two weeks."
Technique: Convert soft stall into specific objection. Then handle that objection.
Objection
"I need to talk to my partner."
Response
"Absolutely — and I want to make that conversation easy. Would it help if I came back for 20 minutes with both of you? I can walk your partner through the same demo and the numbers. That way you're not the one having to defend it on your own. What does next Tuesday or Wednesday look like?"
Technique: Never let "talk to my partner" mean "go home and hope." Put the second meeting on the calendar now.
Objection
"Send me some information and I'll get back to you."
Response
"Happy to. I'll send a one-page summary today — it's the sharpest view of the financials. So I'm not chasing you, let's block 15 minutes next Wednesday afternoon for me to hear your questions. Even if your answer is no, you'll have a real reason rather than it just going cold. Does 2 PM or 4 PM work better?"
Technique: Accept the ask, but anchor the follow-up meeting before you leave.
Objection
"This isn't a priority for us right now."
Response
"Fair. Can I ask — what is the priority right now? Because if revenue capture isn't top of the list, one of two things is probably true: either your practice is already maxed out on all the in-office ancillaries it can run (which is rare), or the priority is about to move. I'd rather we have this conversation 6 months early than 6 months late."
Technique: Reframe "not a priority" as "you may be about to need this." Stay present, not pushy.
Objection
"Call me back in 6 months."
Response
"I'll do that — I'll put you on the calendar for October. But can I ask a quick question: if I come back in six months with exactly this same proposal, what's going to be different about your decision at that point? If the answer is 'nothing really,' then waiting costs you six months of forfeited revenue. If the answer is something specific — great, let's plan against it."
Technique: Accept the delay, surface the real reason, quantify the cost of waiting.
Objection
"We need to wait until next year's budget."
Response
"Let's time this together — but here's a thought. Because this device self-funds from CPT revenue in roughly 6-10 weeks, waiting for a budget cycle actually costs more than starting now. A no-interest payment plan or an operating lease structures this as a monthly operating expense — no capital approval needed. Want me to pull numbers on that?"
Technique: Reframe capital-vs-operating and point to financing.
Objection
"I like it, but I need to run it by the hospital."
Response
"Totally reasonable if you're employed. Let me save you a step — I can provide a packet with clinical validation, HL7 integration spec, service terms, and standard contract language that your hospital supply chain usually asks for. We've been through the approval process at [reference health system]. Who on their side would you want me to copy so we keep the ball moving?"
Technique: Offer to jump into the approval lane with them. Don't let the decision leave your hand.

Close & Commit Playbook
Trial-close language, commitment questions, pilot structures, and how to recognize buying signals
⚠
The close starts at minute one, not minute 25. Every discovery question, every objection response, and every trial close is one piece of the same conversation. If you walk in planning to close, you will. If you walk in hoping the doctor closes themselves, you won't.
1. Trial Closes — Use Early & Often
Temperature checks. If the answer is no, you handle that objection now instead of at the end.
After the live demo
"Assuming the numbers hold up, is this the kind of thing you'd want in your practice?"
Purpose: sort interested vs not-interested early. Any "yes" here is an implicit commitment you can reference at closing.
After the ROI walkthrough
"If your breakeven is really 6 weeks, what would stop you from doing this?"
Purpose: flush out the real objection. Almost always one of: partner approval, capital timing, clinical skepticism, or competitor quote. You handle what surfaces.
When they're nodding along
"Picture this in your exam room next Tuesday. What patient do you run first?"
Purpose: visualization close. Makes the future concrete. A doctor who names a specific patient has mentally bought.
Testing willingness to commit
"If we could solve [their stated blocker], is there any other reason not to move forward?"
Purpose: isolation close. Forces them to either commit conditionally or admit to another hidden objection.
2. Commitment Closes — Asking for the Order
Use after at least one confirming trial close. Do not ask for the order prematurely.
Alternative choice
"Do you want to start with one unit or pilot with two so the satellite location is covered too?"
Purpose: removes the "buy or not" decision and replaces it with "which configuration." Classic.
Assumptive close
"Let me walk Linda through the purchase paperwork while we're all in the room. Best way to make sure nothing gets held up."
Purpose: assumes the decision is made; move straight to execution. Powerful with physicians who are decisive but busy.
Summary close
"Here's where we landed: 8 PFTs a day, $36K device, breakeven in 9 weeks, same CPT codes as the hospital, zero maintenance. Sound right? — Good. Who signs the PO on your side?"
Purpose: recap the deal, get the verbal yes, pivot to logistics.
Urgency without manipulation
"Your next partner meeting is the 15th. If we get the paperwork started by Friday, we can have you up and running in time to bill July patients. Want me to get that going?"
Purpose: ties close date to their own calendar, not a made-up "limited time offer." Respectful urgency.
Pilot structure close
"What if we set this up as a 90-day in-practice pilot — you run the device, track the billing, and at the end of 90 days you either convert to a purchase or send it back? Would that take the decision pressure off?"
Purpose: for practices that can't stomach a one-shot capital decision. Extremely effective with skeptical partners.
3. Buying Signals — Know When to Stop Selling
The #1 rep error is continuing to pitch after the doctor is already bought.
Verbal signal
"When could we get one?" · "Who would train our staff?" · "How fast can you ship?"
Action: STOP selling. Move to logistics. "Great question — we can have the device here in 2-3 weeks once we get the paperwork signed. Let's get that started."
Ownership language
"We'd probably put it in the east exam room." · "Sarah would be the one to run it."
Action: they're visualizing ownership. Reinforce it. "That's a perfect room — Sarah is going to love how fast the workflow is."
Objection intensity drops
Earlier objections were sharp; now they're soft — "I mean, I get it" · "Makes sense."
Action: trial close immediately. The sale is ready; don't miss the window by launching into another feature.
Pulling in others
"Let me grab Linda." · "I want my partner to see this."
Action: they've moved from evaluating to validating. Frame the second person's involvement as confirmation, not re-selling.
Financial specifics
"What's your exact lease rate?" · "Does Section 179 apply if we buy before year-end?"
Action: they're already mentally signed. Pivot to the paperwork path.
4. Never Do These
Every one of these will cost you the deal, sometimes the relationship
Never #1
Don't bad-mouth a competitor by name.
Contrast facts, not character. "NDD Pro can't bill 94726" is a fact; "NDD reps are dishonest" is a stain on you.
Never #2
Don't keep demoing after a buying signal.
If they say "when can we get one," you've already closed. Showing them five more features is you robbing yourself of the order.
Never #3
Don't leave without a specific next step on the calendar.
"I'll follow up" = the deal went cold. "We're meeting Thursday at 2 PM" = the deal has a pulse.
Never #4
Don't discount before the physician asks.
Offering a discount before it's requested signals low value and trains them to expect more. Hold the price. Let them ask.
Never #5
Don't overpromise on reimbursement.
Every ROI slide ends with "subject to specific health plan policy." Stick to that line. A practice that feels misled about payment will never buy again — and they tell their peers.
