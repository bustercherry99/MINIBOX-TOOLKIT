Item one for your call — the CPT bundle

The ToolKit was contradicting itself: the playbook card read 94060 + 94726 + 94729, the estimator read 94010 + 94726 + 94729. I’ve made it consistent on 94010, and here is the sourcing:

NCCI pairs 94060 with 94010 at modifier indicator 0 — meaning no modifier will ever unbundle them, and billing both is a hard denial. 94060 is spirometry with bronchodilator; CMS’s manual describes it as assessing “symptoms that might be related to reversible airway obstruction.” ILD is restrictive, and the ACR/CHEST guideline names the battery as spirometry, lung volumes and DLCO without mentioning bronchodilator testing at all.

There’s also a practical benefit nobody had spotted: 94060 requires direct physician supervision — a doctor in the office suite. 94010, lung volumes and DLCO need only general supervision. So the correct ILD panel is also the easier one for a rheumatology practice to staff. That’s a selling point, not just a correction.

I’d like your sign-off on 94010 as the standing rheumatology bundle before this goes further.

Item two — a coverage gap we should decide how to handle

This one isn’t a coding question and I don’t think it’s mine to answer.

Active Medicare policy states that coverage “excludes routine (screening) tests for asymptomatic patients with or without high risk of lung disease.” The ACR/CHEST guideline recommends screening at-risk patients. Both are true and nothing reconciles them.

The workable line is that a patient with documented lung involvement or respiratory symptoms is being monitored — diagnostic and payable — while a truly asymptomatic patient is being screened, which is patient-liable and needs an ABN. I’ve built that distinction into the estimator so it stops counting unpayable volume as revenue.

Related, and worth knowing before we lean on it: routine PFT surveillance of the asymptomatic methotrexate patient isn’t supported by the evidence base, and the 2021 ACR RA guideline makes no such monitoring recommendation. Baseline testing in a symptomatic patient is defensible; a test-everyone protocol is a weaker story than we’ve been telling.

One more thing worth flagging: Medicare’s standalone PFT coverage policies were retired in November 2023 and folded into the Respiratory Care policy. The frequency figures still circulating — “once annually,” “up to four times a year” — come from that retired document. Several jurisdictions, New York included, now have no PFT-specific policy at all.
